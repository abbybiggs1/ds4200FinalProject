Figures

Figure 1 — Purchase Amount by Category and Gender

Box plot comparing purchase amount distributions across clothing categories, split by gender.

How to read the figure

This figure uses the x axis to represent category (Clothing, Accesories, Footwear, Outerwear), and the y axis represents the purchase amount in USD. The box plot is split by gender to examine whether men or women generally spend more money in any of the categories. You can hover over the plots to get more specific information on quartiles, medians, and mins. You can also click on either male or female to just observe purchase amounts for one gender.

Interpretation

This figure shows minimal variation between men and women in terms of median purchase amount across categories. In addition, purchase amounts tend to be similar for all four categories.

Figure 2 — Purchase Breakdown by Gender and Category

Treemap showing purchase count and amount (USD) per gender and category. Hover over a tile for details.

How to read the figure

In this visualization, we used a tree map to show purchase count and total spend per gender and category. These were the four highest spend categories for both genders (Clothing, Accessories, Footwear, Outerwear). The size of the treemap blocks are based on purchase count, and the hover tool displays the total spend (USD) per category, per gender.

Interpretation

We see that men tend to purchase twice as frequently as women, we also see that their total purchase amounts double as well. Both genders tend to spend the most on clothing, then accessories, then footwear, and lastly outerwear. We were surprised by this data, and were expecting the results to be flipped by gender.

Figure 3 — Item Type Purchased by Season

Stacked bar chart showing how item purchase frequency varies across the four seasons.

How to read the figure

This is a static stacked bar chart showing the frequency of item type purchased in each season from Spring to Winter. The item type is encoded by color as shown on the legend. Use "Shift + Click" to highlight multiple items at once.

Interpretation

While there were no large differences between item type purchased across seasons we did notice slight differences in a few items. For example jackets were purchased more in the Fall, which could suggest that consumers are purchasing jackets at a higher rate because of the cold months that are approaching. Additionally sweaters were purchased less in the summer, which may suggest that there may be a lower demand for layers as the weather starts to warm up.

Figure 4 — Median Purchase Amount by State

Choropleth map of the United States displaying median purchase amount per state.

How to read the figure

This is an interactive choropleth map of the US with a tool tip feature. The saturation of the color indicates the median purchase amount in USD for each of the states. Hover over each state to see their names and their median purchase amounts.

Interpretation

Overall there were no trends with certain regions (eg. East coast vs West coast) having certain types of purchasing patterns. However we noticed that the states with the highest median purchasing amounts were Alaska ($68.50), Arizona ($68.00), and Pennsylvania ($70.00) and the states with the lowest purchasing amounts were Colorado ($51.00), Connecticut ($48.50), Kansas ($50.00), and New Jersey ($51.00).

Figure 5 — Median Purchase Amount by Age Group and Payment Method

Interactive bar and line chart. Click a payment method in the legend to highlight it.

How to read the figure

Here, we are looking at an interactive bar and line chart, with bins of age groups on the x axis, and median purchase amount on the y axis. We also included a tool tip to observe payment method trends. We were curious to see if certain age groups use a payment method more frequently, or if purchase amount might predict payment methods (Bank Transfer, Cash, Credit Card, Debit Card, PayPal, Venmo). You can click on each payment method to highlight the data individually.

Interpretation

Overall, there doesn’t seem to seem to be any significant differences in payment methods for different purchase amounts. We also don’t see any significant changes in payment method by age, however, people 18-25 do tend to use debit cards slightly more than the other groups, and people in higher age groups (56 and up), tend to use Venmo less than the other groups. Suggesting that they use technology slightly less for purchasing than other groups.
